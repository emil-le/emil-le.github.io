Discrete Morse theory and application in topological data analysis.

Chapter 1 Introduction

1.1 Motivation

Topological data analysis (TDA) is field in applied mathematics that challenges the task of extracting and understand information of noisy and incomplete high-dimensional data, which in general is a difficult problem. As both global and local behavior of data are important, TDA aims to find the quantitative topological features that are robust. In differential topology, Morse theory analyzes differential functions (more specifically, the Morse functions) on manifolds to understand the topology of those manifolds. One of the basic results from Morse theory is that most functions on manifolds are Morse functions, and a good choice of a Morse function on a manifold can inform a lot about that manifold. Borrowing both ideas from TDA and Morse theory, discrete Morse theory is constructed in similar way in the combinatoric setting in order to compute the homology or denoise a space.

1.2 Morse Theory

The fundamental idea of Morse theory is the invariant of critical points of a sensible differential function on a manifold, so what is consider a sensible function? In the simplest term, Morse functions are differential functions whose critical points satisfying some specific conditions. Before we get into this idea, let’s consider this example below to see that how the critical points are important information of a manifold.

\[\text{Figure 1.1: Critical points of a terrain.}\] Example Let’s consider a unit sphere \(S^2\subset \mathbb{R}^3\) with a function that collapse the sphere into the \(z-\)axis: \[ \begin{aligned} S^2 = \{(x,y,z)&\vert x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1\} \\ \\ f\text{ }: S^2 &\longrightarrow \mathbb{R} \\ (x,y,z)&\longmapsto z \end{aligned} \] The gradient of \(f\) is \(\nabla f = [0 \text{ } 0 \text{ }1]^\text{T}\). Let \(m\in\mathbb{R}\), we can see that the critical point of \(f\) encodes information about the sub-level set of \(S^2\):

Here, we can see that the critical points of the \(2-\)sphere and \(2-\)torus with its sub-level set from the height function can tell the 2 spaces apart from the number of \(n-\)dimensional holes. Intuitively, these are the specific topological signature of some shapes in spaces. Here, let’s recall some key definition of critical points in a differential manifold setting.

Definition (Manifold) An \(n-\)manifold \(\mathcal{M}\subset\mathbb{R}^n\) is a \(T_2\) topological space (Hausdorff and with countable basis), where there exists a neighborhood \(U\subseteq \mathcal{M}\) for every \(m\in\mathcal{M}\) such that \(U\) is homeomorphic to an open ball in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) induced by the Eucledian metric.

Note The definition of manifold in topology refers to manifold with boundary, and manifold without boundary are not manifold.

\[\text{Figure 1.5: The torus is a manifold but the mobius strip is not.}\]

Definition (Critical Point) For an \(n-\)manifold \(\mathcal{M}\subset\mathbb{R}^n\), let \(f: \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}\). An critical point of \(f\) is \(x\in\mathcal{M}\) such that \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 0\)

\[ \begin{aligned}\text{Figure 1.6:} &\text{ (Top row) Minimum critical point and maximum critical point.}\\ &\text{ (Bottom row) Saddle critical point.} \end{aligned} \]

Intuitively, a Morse function is a function maps a manifold domain to \(\mathbb{R}\) with all critical points are non-degenerative (local maximum, minimum, or saddle point). However, a function on a manifold domain does not have second-derivative, but we can define it on local point.

Definition (Nondegenerative) Let \(x \in \mathcal{M}\) be a critical point of \(f: \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}\). Consider the tangent space at \(x\), \(T_x\mathcal{M}\), let \(\vec{\mathbf{u}},\vec{\mathbf{v}} \in T_x\mathcal{M}\) be arbitrary. Define \(\partial^{2} f_x(\vec{\mathbf{u}},\vec{\mathbf{v}}) = \vec{\mathbf{u}}(\bar{\vec{\mathbf{v}}}f)(x)\) (where \(\bar{\vec{\mathbf{v}}}\) is an local extension of \(\vec{v}\)), then the Hessians for \(f\) is given by: \[ \begin{align} \mathbf{H}_{f_{ij}} &\equiv \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x_{i} \partial x_{j} } \\ \mathbf{H}_{f} &= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x_{1}^2} & \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x_{1} \partial x_{2} } & \dots & \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x_{1} \partial x_{n} } \\ \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x_{2} \partial x_{1} } & \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x_{2}^2 } & \dots & \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x_{2} \partial x_{n} } \\ \vdots & \vdots &\ddots & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x_{n}\partial x_{1}} & \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x_{n} \partial x_{2} } & \dots & \frac{\partial^{2} f}{\partial x_{n}^2 } \end{bmatrix} \end{align} \] The critical point \(x\) is non-degenerative if its Hessian matrix is invertible.

Definition (Morse Function) A Morse function is a function that the Hessian value of every critical point is non-degenerative.

There are several interesting results from Morse theory that are out of scope for the project. However, informally speaking, one of the relevant finding is that most functions are Morse function. Recall in calculus III, we can approximate a function by its Taylor expansion to the second derivative at a point \(a\) in the domain: \[f(x) \approx f(a) + \frac{1}{1!}f'(a)(x-a) + \frac{1}{2!}f''(a)(x-a)^2\] Then the Morse lemma states that for a Morse function \(f\) with critical point \(a\), then there exists a local chart \((x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n)\) such that: \[f(x) = f(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n) = f(a) - \sum_{i=1}^jx_i^2 + \sum_{i=j+1}^nx_i^2 \] Furthermore, for a compact manifold \(\mathcal{M}\), its critical values are only dependent on \(\mathcal{M}\) regardless of choices for the Morse function. There are many fascinating applications of Morse theory in dynamics system, density estimation, etc, however, this project focuses on the discrete version of Morse theory. Namely, we apply the similar idea from persistence homology to find the persistence critical points from the triangulation of our inputs.

1.3 Algebraic topology

In simplest term, topologists differentiate abstract spaces by finding its homeomorphic invariants such as compactness, Hausdorff property, connectivity, etc. Algebraic topology turns abstract topological space into combinatorical object to study and compute topological invariant in “holes” of a space using linear algbera.

Definition A \(k-\)simplex \(\Delta = [v_0,v_1\dots,v_n]\) is the smallest convex subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) containing \(k+1\) points which are affinitely independent.

\[ \begin{aligned}\text{Figure 1.7:} &\text{ From left to right respectively, a 0-simpliex (a point), 1-simplex (an edge),}\\ &\text{ 2-simplex (a filled triangle) and 3-simplex (a filled tetrahedron).} \end{aligned} \]
Morse theory study a another type of invariant of differentiable manifold by looking at its differential function, specifically its critical values, defined on that space. Discrete Morse theory uses the same idea by turning these gradient field into “simpler” complexes that can represent the topological space.
