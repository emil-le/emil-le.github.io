4.1 Motivation

In neuroscience, Reinagel (1999) points out that humanâ€™s eyes moves more rapidly around high-contrast regions when looking at an image. Thus, a rational assumption about the natural images is that the high-contrast region areas encode the most significant relevant information about an image. Denoising an image is an important challenge in the field of computer science. As researchers tend to believe that low-contrast regions (noise) of an image follows a non-Gaussian distribution. Often time, image analysis problem takes spherical projection of visual data as it encapsules the complicated geometric structure of images (for example, luminosity, folow, \(\dots\)). This motivates finding statistical methods to estimate the noise non-Gaussian distribution of natural images on a Riemannian domain.

4.2 k-nearest neighbor

The k-nearest neighbor (k-NN) is one of fundamental method for non-parametric estimation, which intuitively makes a lot of sense to think about it. Assume that our data follows has a non-Gaussian distribution \(f\), then the distribution of the \(k-\)NN of our data (for some \(k\)) is inversely proportional to \(f\). Why? Itâ€™s because a value on the distribution of \(k-\)NN represents there are at least \(k\) points lies near a point in our data set, so the inverse would represents the density around that point. The problem here translates to, what is the best choice for \(k\)?

Defintion Let \(\mathcal{X_1},\mathcal{X_2},\dots\mathcal{X}_n\) be \(n\) i.i.d. r.v. on Riemannian manifold space \((\mathcal{M},\rho)\) (\(\mathcal{M}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}\)) with an assumed distribution \(f\). An estimator for \(f\) using k-nearest neighbor methods proposed by Wagner (1975) is given by: \[\hat f_n(p)= \frac{1}{nH_\rho(p)}\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{1}{V_{\mathcal{X}_i}(p)}K(\frac{\rho(p,\mathcal{X}_i)}{H_\rho(p)}), \text{ where}:\] \[ \begin{cases} K:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R} \text{ is a non-negative function with compact support}\\ V_{q}(p) \text{ is a volumn density function on } (\mathcal{M},\rho) \\ H_\rho(p) \text{ is the distance induced by }\rho \text{ representing the nearest k-neighbors of } p\\ \end{cases} \] In general, we can think of \(\hat f_n(p)\propto\frac{1}{\text{Volumn of }S^{n-1}\text{ of radius }H_\rho(p)}\)
