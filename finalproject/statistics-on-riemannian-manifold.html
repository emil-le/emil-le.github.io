Chapter 2 Statistics on Riemannian Manifold

2.1 Riemannian Manifold

Definiton Let \(T_p\mathcal{M}\) be the tangent space of a point \(p\) in an differentiable manifold \(\mathcal{M}\subset \mathbb{R}^n\). The Riemannian manifold is defined as the topological space on \(\mathcal{M}\) induced by the Riemannian metric \(\rho\) by: \[ \begin{aligned} \langle ., .\rangle_p: T_p\mathcal{M}\times T_p\mathcal{M} &\rightarrow \mathbb{R}\\ \vec{\mathbf{v}} &\longmapsto \sqrt{\langle \vec{\mathbf{v}},\vec{\mathbf{v}} \rangle_p}\\ \end{aligned} \] Definiton A curve in \(\mathcal{M}\) is defined as \(\gamma: [0,1]\to \mathcal{M}\) such that \(\gamma\) is differentiable. The length of a curve is defined as \[L(\gamma) = \int_a^b \sqrt{\langle \gamma(t)^{'} \rangle_{\gamma(t)}} dt\] Definiton The geodesics between arbitrary \(x,y\in\mathcal{M}\) is given by the metric \(\rho: \mathcal{M}\times \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}\): \[ \rho(x,y) = \inf\{L(\gamma) \vert \gamma(0) = x, \gamma(1) = y)\} \]

For each point \(p\in\mathcal{M}\) and \(\vec{\mathbf{v}}\in T_p\mathcal{M}\), there exists a unique geodesic \(\gamma: [0,1]\to\mathcal{M}\) such that \(\gamma(0)=p\) and \(\gamma^{'}(1)=\vec{\mathbf{v}}\). The exponential map \(\exp_p : T_p\mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{M}\) is defined as \(\exp_p(\vec{v}) = \gamma(p)\)

2.2 Instrinsic mean and variance:

Definiton Let \(\mathcal{X}\) be a random variable on \((\mathcal{M},\rho)\) with propability measure \(\mathbb{P}\) and expectation operand \(\mathbb{E}_\mathcal{X}[.]\), the instrinsic mean and variance is defined as: \[\mu_{I} = \arg\inf_{m\in\mathcal{M}} \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{X}}[\rho^2(\mathcal{X},m)]\] \[\sigma^2_{I}= \inf_{m\in\mathcal{M}} \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{X}}[\rho^2(\mathcal{X},m)]\] If \(\mu_I\) is the global minimum of \(\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{X}}[\rho^2(\mathcal{X},m)]\), then \(\mu_I\) is called a Fréchet mean. In statistical manifold, the existence of Fréchet mean is more emphasized.

Theorem Let \((\mathcal{M},\rho)\) be a Riemannian manifold. The Fréchet mean function \(\mathcal{F}:\mathcal{M}\to\mathbb{R}\) is defined as: \[\mathcal{F}(x)=\int_\mathcal{M} \rho(p,x)dx \] The sufficient condition for the existence of Fréchet mean in a Riemannian manifold \(\mathcal{M}\) is if \(\mathcal{M}\) is complete and \(\mathcal{F}(p)\) is finite for some \(p\).

2.3 Sample Fréchet mean and variance:

Definiton Let \(X = \{x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n\}\subset\mathcal{M}\) be a finite collection of \(n\) points on a complete Riemannian space \((\mathcal{M},\rho)\), the Sample Fréchet mean and variance is given by: \[\hat\mu_F = \arg\min_{m\in\mathcal{M}} \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \rho^2(m,x_i)\] \[\hat\sigma_F = \min_{m\in\mathcal{M}} \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \rho^2(m,x_i)\] We need to prove that \(\hat\mu_F\) and \(\hat\sigma_F\) exist.

Proof Let \(X = \{x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n\}\subset\mathcal{M}\) be a finite collection of \(n\) points on a complete Riemannian space \((\mathcal{M},\rho)\). Let \(\mathcal{F}: \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}\) be given by \[\mathcal{F}(m) = \sum_{i=1}^n \rho^2(m,x_i)\] It is sufficient to show that \(\mathcal{F}(m)\) achieves a minimum. Let \(\epsilon = \max\{\rho(x_i,x_j)\vert i\neq j\}\) be the maximum distance, consider the finite union of closed \(\rho-\)ball cenetered at \(x_i\) \(\bar{B}_\rho(\epsilon,x_i)\) for all \(x_i\in X\): \[C = \bigcup_{i=1}^n \bar{B}_\rho(\epsilon,x_i)\] It follows that \(C\) is a closed and bounded subset of the complete metric space \(\mathcal{M}\), which implies \(C\) is compact. For arbitrary \(x\in C\) and \(\varepsilon >0\) arbitrary, let there be an open Eucledian ball \(B_d(\varepsilon,\mathcal{F}(x))\) of \(\mathcal{F}(x)\). Then \(\mathcal{F}^{-1}(B_d(\varepsilon,\mathcal{F}(x))\) is an neighborhood of \(x\) in \(\mathcal{M}\), pick \(\delta > 0\) such that \(\delta < \frac{\epsilon}{2}\). For arbitrary \(m'\in \mathcal{F}^{-1}(B_d(\varepsilon,\mathcal{F}(x))\), the open ball \(B_\rho(\delta,m')\subset \mathcal{F}^{-1}(B_d(\varepsilon,\mathcal{F}(x)))\) so pre-image of open Eucledian ball is open in \(\mathcal{M}\). By Extreme Value Theorem, the \(\mathcal{F}\) has a global minimum.
